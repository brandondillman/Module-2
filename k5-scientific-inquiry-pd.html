Scientific Inquiry Teaching Strategies

A Professional Development Module for K-5 Educators

Understanding Scientific Inquiry in Elementary Education

Scientific inquiry is an investigative, hands-on approach where young students actively explore the natural world by asking questions, making observations, and conducting simple experiments to construct evidence-based explanations.

Key Aspects of Scientific Inquiry for Elementary Students

  • Student-Led Questions: Learning begins with questions that are meaningful and researchable, allowing students to drive their own learning.
  • Hands-On Exploration: Children engage in activities to investigate problems, test ideas, and observe phenomena firsthand.
  • Evidence-Based Explanations: Students learn to use evidence gathered from their observations and experiments to formulate explanations and draw conclusions.
  • Teacher as Facilitator: Instead of direct instruction, teachers guide and support students, providing opportunities for them to explore, discover, and construct their own understanding.
  • Development of Critical Skills: Inquiry fosters critical thinking, problem-solving, data analysis, and the ability to assess and communicate findings.

The Scientific Inquiry Process

  1. Ask Questions: Students ask questions about the world around them
  2. Investigate & Experiment: They design and conduct simple experiments and make observations
  3. Analyze Data: Children organize and analyze the information they have collected
  4. Formulate Explanations: Based on evidence, students create their own explanations and conclusions
  5. Communicate & Justify: Students share their findings and explain how their evidence supports their conclusions

Foundations of Inquiry-Based Teaching

Shifting Your Role as Teacher

From: Information provider, direct instructor

To: Facilitator, guide, co-investigator

  • Ask open-ended questions rather than giving answers
  • Encourage student thinking with prompts like "What do you notice?" and "What makes you think that?"
  • Allow productive struggle and mistakes as learning opportunities

Creating an Inquiry-Rich Environment

  • Physical Space: Flexible seating, easy access to materials, investigation stations
  • Culture of Questioning: Celebrate curiosity, display student questions prominently
  • Time for Wonder: Build in time for observation and exploration before formal lessons
  • Materials: Simple, open-ended materials that invite exploration

Supporting Student Questions

  • Model good questioning through your own wondering aloud
  • Help students refine questions to make them testable
  • Use a "Question Board" or "Wonder Wall" to collect and revisit questions
  • Teach the difference between questions that can be researched vs. tested

Kindergarten Inquiry Strategies

Kindergarten Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • Matter and Interactions: Physical properties (size, color, shape, texture, weight, flexibility)
  • Motion and Forces: Position words, movement patterns
  • Living vs. Nonliving: Characteristics, structures, basic needs
  • Weather Patterns: Daily and seasonal changes
Inquiry Example: "What makes things move?"

Student Questions: "Why do some balls roll farther?" "What happens if I push harder?"

Investigation: Students explore different objects (balls, blocks, cars) on various surfaces, documenting how far each travels with different amounts of force.

Teacher Role: Ask "What do you notice about...?" "How could we test that idea?" "What would happen if...?"

Evidence Collection: Simple drawings, measuring with non-standard units (paper clips, blocks)

Age-Appropriate Inquiry Strategies

  • Sensory Explorations: Use all five senses safely to observe and describe
  • Sorting and Classifying: Group objects by observable properties
  • Simple Predictions: "What do you think will happen if...?"
  • Drawing Observations: Pictures with simple labels or verbal descriptions
  • Pattern Recognition: "What do you notice that's the same/different?"
Inquiry Example: "Living or Not Living?"

Student Questions: "Is a seed alive?" "How do we know if something is living?"

Investigation: Students observe various objects (plants, toys, animals, rocks) and develop criteria for "living" based on their observations.

Evidence Collection: Observation journals with drawings and simple check-lists

Grade 1 Inquiry Strategies

Grade 1 Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • Forces and Motion: Pushes and pulls, contact vs. non-contact forces
  • Plant and Animal Needs: Basic needs in different environments
  • Earth/Moon/Sun System: Day/night sky differences, moon patterns
  • Earth Materials: Properties of rocks, minerals, soils, water
  • Natural Resources: Human uses and protection
Inquiry Example: "How do different soils affect plant growth?"

Student Questions: "Which soil is best for plants?" "Why do some plants grow taller?"

Investigation: Students plant seeds in different types of soil samples, create fair tests by controlling variables, and document plant growth over time.

Teacher Role: Guide discussions about fair testing: "What should we keep the same?" "What will we change?"

Evidence Collection: Daily measurement charts, growth drawings, simple data tables

Grade 1 Inquiry Developments

  • Fair Test Introduction: Begin discussing what makes a test fair
  • Simple Data Collection: Use charts, tallies, and basic measurements
  • Cause and Effect: "Because...then..." reasoning
  • Comparison Skills: More than/less than, same/different
  • Beginning Communication: Share findings with simple explanations
Inquiry Example: "What affects how far things are pushed?"

Student Questions: "Does the size of the push matter?" "Do heavy things need bigger pushes?"

Investigation: Students use ramps and various objects, testing how distance of push affects how far objects travel.

Evidence Collection: Measuring distances, recording results in simple charts

Grade 2 Inquiry Strategies

Grade 2 Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • Matter States and Changes: Solids/liquids, freezing/melting, evaporation
  • Sound and Vibration: How sound is produced and detected
  • Animal Life Cycles: Birth, growth, reproduction, aging, death
  • Heredity: Similarities and differences between parents and offspring
  • Weather Patterns: Energy from sun, weather tools, seasonal patterns
Inquiry Example: "What happens to water in different containers?"

Student Questions: "Where does water go when it disappears?" "Does shape of container matter?"

Investigation: Students place equal amounts of water in different shaped containers (wide, narrow, covered, uncovered) and measure changes over time.

Teacher Role: Help students design controlled experiments and make detailed observations

Evidence Collection: Daily measurements, weather data correlation, detailed observation logs

Grade 2 Inquiry Advancements

  • Variable Control: Understanding what to change and what to keep the same
  • Quantitative Data: Using simple measurements and tools
  • Pattern Recognition: Identifying trends in data over time
  • Model Making: Creating simple diagrams and models to explain
  • Comparing Results: Looking for similarities and differences across investigations
Inquiry Example: "How do different animals' offspring compare to their parents?"

Student Questions: "Why don't all puppies look exactly like their mom?" "What makes children similar to their parents?"

Investigation: Students observe photos/videos of various animals and their offspring, creating comparison charts of similarities and differences.

Evidence Collection: Comparison charts, pattern documentation, family tree diagrams

Grade 3 Inquiry Strategies

Grade 3 Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • States of Matter: Properties of solids, liquids, gases; changes due to heating/cooling
  • Forces and Motion: Speed, direction, gravity effects
  • Energy Transfer: Heat creation through friction, energy movement
  • Human Body Systems: Skeletal, muscular, skin functions
  • Plant Structures: Functions of plant parts, life cycles
  • Environmental Factors: How environment affects plant survival
  • Earth's Features: Land and water features, solar system patterns
Inquiry Example: "What factors affect how fast objects fall?"

Student Questions: "Do heavier things always fall faster?" "Why do feathers fall slowly?"

Investigation: Students drop various objects (different weights, shapes, sizes) and measure fall times, testing in normal conditions and with fans.

Teacher Role: Guide students to identify multiple variables and help them plan controlled tests

Evidence Collection: Timing data, video analysis, hypothesis testing charts

Grade 3 Inquiry Sophistication

  • Multiple Variable Testing: Understanding how different factors affect outcomes
  • Hypothesis Formation: Making testable predictions with reasoning
  • Data Analysis: Looking for patterns, trends, and outliers in data
  • Model Creation: Building and using models to explain phenomena
  • Scientific Communication: Presenting findings with evidence-based arguments
Inquiry Example: "What do plants need to grow best?"

Student Questions: "Do plants need light?" "How much water is too much?" "What happens without soil?"

Investigation: Students design multi-variable experiments testing light, water, soil type, and nutrients, controlling variables across multiple plant groups.

Evidence Collection: Growth measurements, photographic documentation, data tables, conclusion statements

Grade 4 Inquiry Strategies

Grade 4 Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • Energy Forms: Light, sound, heat, electrical energy; circuits and conductors
  • Magnetic and Electric Forces: Non-contact forces, charged object interactions
  • Light Behavior: Reflection, refraction, absorption
  • Animal Adaptations: Structural and behavioral adaptations for survival
  • Fossils and Earth History: Fossil formation, Earth's changing environment
  • Earth's Systems: Day/night causes, moon phases, rock and mineral properties
  • Human Environmental Impact: Changes to environment, adaptation strategies
Inquiry Example: "How do different materials affect the brightness of bulbs in circuits?"

Student Questions: "Which materials let electricity through best?" "Why do some bulbs shine brighter?"

Investigation: Students create circuits with different materials (copper wire, aluminum foil, paper clips, rubber bands) and measure/compare bulb brightness.

Teacher Role: Guide safety procedures, help students develop systematic testing approaches

Evidence Collection: Circuit diagrams, brightness ratings, conductor/insulator classifications, explanatory writing

Grade 4 Inquiry Complexity

  • System Thinking: Understanding how parts work together in systems
  • Abstract Reasoning: Connecting observations to invisible phenomena (electricity, forces)
  • Evidence-Based Arguments: Using data to support claims and counter-arguments
  • Design Thinking: Engineering solutions based on scientific principles
  • Cross-Cutting Concepts: Recognizing patterns, cause-effect, energy across topics
Inquiry Example: "How do animal structures help them survive in different environments?"

Student Questions: "Why do desert animals have big ears?" "How do bird beaks match their food?"

Investigation: Students research and model different animal structures, then design and test artificial structures that solve similar survival problems.

Evidence Collection: Research data, design sketches, test results, comparison charts, presentation materials

Grade 5 Inquiry Strategies

Grade 5 Focus Areas (Based on NC Standards)

  • Matter Interactions: Chemical vs. physical changes, conservation of matter
  • Forces and Motion: Gravity, friction, mass effects on motion
  • Human Body Systems: Organizational levels, system interactions
  • Ecosystems: Producer/consumer relationships, ecosystem interdependence
  • Inherited vs. Acquired Traits: Instincts vs. learned behaviors
  • Weather and Climate Systems: Atmospheric systems, water cycle, severe weather
Inquiry Example: "What happens to matter during chemical changes?"

Student Questions: "Does matter disappear when it changes?" "How can we prove matter is conserved?"

Investigation: Students conduct controlled experiments mixing substances (baking soda & vinegar, burning candles in closed systems) and measure masses before and after reactions.

Teacher Role: Facilitate discussions about molecular-level changes, help students design precise measurement procedures

Evidence Collection: Mass measurements, chemical change indicators, molecular models, scientific explanations

Grade 5 Inquiry Mastery

  • Independent Investigation Design: Students plan complete investigations with minimal guidance
  • Complex Data Analysis: Interpreting multi-variable data, identifying outliers and sources of error
  • Scientific Modeling: Creating and revising models to explain phenomena
  • Peer Review: Evaluating and improving others' investigations and explanations
  • Real-World Applications: Connecting classroom investigations to current scientific issues
Inquiry Example: "How do changes in one part of an ecosystem affect the whole system?"

Student Questions: "What happens if predators disappear?" "How do plants affect the animals that eat them?"

Investigation: Students design ecosystem models, manipulate variables (remove species, change resources), and track effects on population balances over time.

Evidence Collection: Population data, food web diagrams, ecosystem balance charts, predictive models, environmental impact reports

Implementation Strategies

Starting Small: Your First Steps

  • Week 1: Add 10 minutes of "wonder time" to one science lesson
  • Week 2: Create a "Question Board" and have students post one question daily
  • Week 3: Try one simple investigation where students collect their own data
  • Week 4: Have students share findings and explain their thinking to peers

Common Challenges and Solutions

Challenge: "Students ask questions I can't answer"

Solution: Model being a learner - "Let's investigate that together!" or "How could we find out?"

Challenge: "Investigations take too much time"

Solution: Start with 15-minute mini-investigations, use simple materials, focus on one variable

Challenge: "Students struggle with open-ended tasks"

Solution: Provide structure with templates, choice boards, or guided questions while maintaining student ownership

Assessment in Inquiry-Based Learning

  • Process Focus: Assess thinking and reasoning, not just right answers
  • Student Voice: Include self-reflection and peer feedback
  • Evidence Collection: Use science notebooks, observation checklists, video documentation
  • Growth Mindset: Celebrate improved questioning, thinking, and investigation skills

Building Administrator and Parent Support

  • Share student work that demonstrates deep thinking and engagement
  • Explain how inquiry aligns with standards and develops critical life skills
  • Invite stakeholders to observe inquiry lessons in action
  • Document and communicate student growth in scientific thinking
